Introduction

In today’s digital world, the value of data cannot be overstated. But in research, where data often includes highly sensitive personal information, the stakes are even higher. This is why data security isn’t just a compliance issue—it’s an ethical one.

This talk aims to provide a foundational understanding of research data security, its unique challenges, and how aligning practices with industry standards can help mitigate risks.

We will cover the following topics:

  • Understanding Research Data as an Institutional Asset
  • Key Types of Sensitive Information in Research
  • Institutional Information Security Controls and Standards
  • Federal Guidelines and Legal Compliance
  • Best Practices for Securing Research Data
  • Challenges and Considerations
  • Call to Action: Strengthening Data Security and Data Integrity in Research

Understanding Research Data as an Institutional Asset

Research data, including experimental data, survey results, and digital health records, often contains highly sensitive elements like Personally Identifiable Information (PII) and Protected Health Information (PHI).

Research data should be treated as a critical institutional asset, with implications for compliance and data governance. The demand for quality research data has risen with AI and analytics, making data integrity crucial for reliable insights.

Key Types of Sensitive Information in Research

Research often involves sensitive data types, including PII and PHI. De-identified data can still present risks of re-identification through linkage with other datasets.

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

PII includes names, addresses, social security numbers, and biometric data. Exposure can lead to identity theft and legal implications under regulations like GDPR and CCPA.

Protected Health Information (PHI)

PHI includes medical records and health data, governed by HIPAA. Handling PHI requires strict measures for consent, storage, and data sharing to comply with legal standards.

Institutional Information Security Controls and Standards

Institutional security controls protect data from unauthorized access and ensure compliance. Common frameworks include:

  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework
  • ISO/IEC 27001 Information Security Management System

Core security practices include data classification, role-based access control, encryption, and incident response planning.

Federal Guidelines and Legal Compliance

Federal regulations like HIPAA, FERPA, and FISMA set standards for research data protection. Aligning with NIST guidelines strengthens institutional security frameworks and compliance.

Best Practices for Securing Research Data

  • Vet and integrate secure systems throughout the data lifecycle.
  • Use robust APIs for seamless data exchange.
  • Minimize manual data handoffs and avoid local downloads of sensitive data.
  • Leverage virtualized environments for secure data analysis.
  • Avoid using spreadsheets for data management; use integrated tools instead.

Challenges and Considerations

Balancing data access with security, overcoming resource constraints, and managing legacy systems are common challenges. A unified data governance approach can mitigate these issues.

Call to Action

Research data is a strategic asset. Investing in secure infrastructure and fostering a culture of security and integrity are key to safeguarding data. Strong leadership and collaboration across teams are essential to support these initiatives.
