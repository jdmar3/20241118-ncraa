Key types of sensitive information in research

Start by acknowledging that sensitive information in research often overlaps with regulated data types, including PII and PHI.

Emphasize the unique challenges in research environments, where datasets may be extensive and de-identified data can still present risks if re-identification occurs.

Overview

Identity: PII

Define PII as any information that can be used to distinguish or trace an individual’s identity, either directly or indirectly.

Examples of PII: Names, addresses, social security numbers, IP addresses, email addresses, and even biometric data.

Risks of Exposure: Identity theft, financial fraud, and loss of privacy. Discuss how unauthorized access to PII can lead to legal implications, especially under regulations like GDPR and CCPA.

Considerations for Research Data: Explain how PII often appears in survey data, participant registries, or even metadata, requiring robust measures to anonymize or secure it.

Health: PHI

Define PHI in the context of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA). PHI includes any health-related information tied to an individual’s identity.

Examples of PHI: Medical records, health insurance information, lab results, and any data collected through health research that can be linked to an individual.

Legal and Ethical Implications: Highlight the strict requirements under HIPAA for handling PHI, including the need for proper consent, secure storage, and limitations on data sharing.

Mention potential fines and legal actions for breaches.

Deductive disclosure

Even when datasets are anonymized, there is a risk of deductive disclosure, where participants can be identified through a combination of variables or through linking datasets.

This is especially concerning in small sample studies or when dealing with unique populations.

Emphasize the importance of careful data analysis and sharing practices to mitigate this risk.

"Unintended consequences"

Address how PII and PHI often overlap in research contexts, particularly in clinical and biomedical studies.

Discuss the need for extra precautions when handling datasets that contain both PII and PHI, as these datasets are considered highly sensitive and have more stringent compliance requirements.

Mention new forms of sensitive data, such as genetic information or digital behavioral data (e.g., activity from wearables), which are becoming more common in research and present new privacy challenges.

Data classification:

Emphasize the importance of classifying data based on its sensitivity (e.g., public, internal, restricted, or confidential) to guide appropriate security measures.

Access Control:

Discuss role-based access control (RBAC), which restricts data access based on the user’s role within the organization. Highlight the principle of least privilege, ensuring users have the minimum access necessary for their work.

Encryption:

Explain the importance of encrypting sensitive data, both at rest and in transit, to protect against unauthorized access. Mention that encryption should be aligned with industry standards (e.g., AES-256 for strong encryption).

Incident Response and Monitoring:

Stress the need for robust incident response plans to quickly address data breaches or security incidents. Mention the role of continuous monitoring and logging to detect unusual activity.

Introduce FISMA as the legislation governing the protection of Federal information systems. Explain that FISMA requires organizations to implement a comprehensive information security program, particularly when dealing with Federal research grants or projects involving government data.

Improving practices for securing research data

System vetting

Emphasize that the foundation of a secure research data ecosystem begins with carefully vetting and selecting systems for each stage of the data lifecycle. This involves assessing the security features of potential tools and ensuring they comply with institutional and Federal standards for handling sensitive data.

Minimizing manual handoffs

Discuss the dangers of manual data handoffs and the transfer of data to personal devices, which are significant security risks. These practices create vulnerable points where data can be mishandled, lost, or exposed, particularly when dealing with PII or PHI.

Emphasize that a well-integrated ecosystem reduces the need for researchers to download and manually transfer data, helping to maintain the security and integrity of sensitive information.

Secure virtualized environments

Recommend the use of virtualized environments that provide analytical tools and secure access to data within the ecosystem. This approach encourages researchers and research teams to work with data directly in a secure, controlled environment, rather than downloading it locally.

Explain that this strategy is particularly critical for datasets containing PII or PHI, as it minimizes the risk of unauthorized access or accidental exposure.

Mention the added benefit of improved data integrity, as virtualized environments can log and track all data interactions, reducing the opportunity for untracked edits or manipulations.

Avoiding spreadsheets

Provide a negative example of using spreadsheets for data editing, organizing, or managing. Explain that spreadsheets are prone to human error, lack robust version control, and do not capture the full history of data changes, making them unsuitable for handling sensitive research data.

Stress that instead of using spreadsheets, researchers should leverage integrated data management tools within the ecosystem that offer secure, auditable, and collaborative features.

Ensuring data integrity

Reinforce the importance of maintaining secure data pipelines that protect data throughout its lifecycle—from collection and storage to analysis and sharing. Secure integration reduces the risk of data breaches and supports consistent data quality.

Monitoring and audits

Recommend ongoing monitoring of data access and activity across the ecosystem. Implementing logging and audit capabilities within integrated systems helps detect unusual activities early and provides a transparent record of data interactions.

Training and awareness

Highlight the need for training programs to ensure that all users of the ecosystem understand best practices for data security and integrity. Training should cover secure data handling, the importance of using ecosystem tools rather than local downloads, and awareness of emerging threats.

Challenges and Considerations

Balancing Access and Security

One of the primary challenges in research data security is finding the right balance between making data accessible for analysis and collaboration while ensuring it remains secure. This is especially complex in multi-institutional projects involving external partners with varying security capabilities.

Scalable access controls are essential to accommodate different data sensitivity levels and user roles without compromising security.

Complacency: "It won't happen here."

Address the common perception in academia that research data is less likely to be targeted by malicious actors. This mindset can lead to complacency and underinvestment in security measures.

Use examples of data breaches in research settings to illustrate that sensitive datasets, particularly those valuable for AI and machine learning, are prime targets for attackers.

Resource constraints and competing priorities

Limited budget and personnel can make it challenging for institutions to implement comprehensive data security measures. Security often competes with immediate research needs and project timelines.

Suggest prioritizing critical investments, such as cloud-based security services and collaboration with institutional IT, to optimize resources.

Uneven data governance

Highlight that inconsistent data governance practices across departments or projects can create significant vulnerabilities. Often, data ownership is unclear, leading to a lack of standardized policies for data handling, access, and retention.

Emphasize the need for a unified data governance framework that clearly defines data ownership, responsibilities, and standardized protocols for managing research data securely across the institution.

Evolving threats

The threat landscape is constantly changing, with new vulnerabilities and attack methods emerging. Research institutions must remain agile and proactive to address threats like phishing, ransomware, and insider attacks, which can be particularly damaging when sensitive data is compromised

Legacy systems

Many research institutions rely on legacy systems that may not meet modern security standards. Integrating these outdated systems into a secure ecosystem can be difficult but is necessary for comprehensive data protection.

Recommend conducting regular assessments of legacy systems to identify security gaps and prioritize necessary upgrades or replacements.

Compliance complexity

Complying with multiple regulations (e.g., HIPAA, FERPA, GDPR) can be complex, especially in international projects or multi-site studies. This complexity often leads to fragmented compliance efforts and potential oversights.

Suggest the creation of unified data governance policies that align with the most stringent applicable standards, simplifying compliance across projects.

Data integrity in collaborative environments

Collaborative research often involves multiple stakeholders accessing and modifying data. Ensuring data integrity requires robust version control and audit trails.

Emphasize the importance of centralized data management tools that track changes and provide a clear, auditable history of data interactions.

Call to Action

Recognize data as a strategic asset

Reiterate the central theme: Research data is a critical institutional asset whose value extends beyond individual projects. Its integrity and security are fundamental not only to compliance but to the institution’s reputation, research credibility, and its contributions to AI and machine learning.

Invest in secure infrastructure

Emphasize the need for institutions to invest in secure data infrastructure, focusing on tools and systems that protect data at every stage of its lifecycle. Secure storage, identity management, and continuous monitoring are essential not just for preventing breaches but for maintaining data accuracy and reliability.

Highlight that investments in technology and training are vital to support both data security and data integrity. Well-trained staff are key to implementing processes that prevent unauthorized changes and ensure data remains consistent and accurate.

Build a culture of research data security and integrity

Stress the importance of fostering a culture where data security and data integrity are seen as intertwined responsibilities. Embedding data security practices into everyday research activities helps maintain the accuracy, completeness, and reliability of the data.

Recommend regular training sessions that emphasize the dual goals of protecting data from external threats and ensuring that any internal data manipulation is transparent and properly documented.

Foster collaboration between research and IT teams

Encourage stronger collaboration between research and IT teams to develop solutions that address both security and integrity needs. Effective collaboration helps design systems that prevent unauthorized data access and support rigorous version control and auditing.

Suggest forming a cross-functional data security and integrity committee with representatives from research, IT, and compliance to oversee the implementation of measures that uphold both principles

Standardize data governance

Advocate for clear, standardized data governance policies that apply across all departments. These policies should outline protocols for secure data handling and integrity checks, reducing inconsistencies and ensuring that data remains accurate and protected throughout its lifecycle.

Emphasize that standardized governance not only aids in compliance but also ensures that data quality is maintained, making it easier to trace and verify data origins and modifications.

Urge institutions to be proactive in adapting security measures to the evolving threat landscape while also implementing checks that safeguard data integrity. Regular updates to protocols and risk assessments help prevent breaches and detect potential data inaccuracies early.

Highlight the value of participating in threat intelligence sharing networks with other research institutions to stay informed about emerging risks that could compromise both data security and integrity.

Research data leadership

Emphasize that institutional leadership is crucial in prioritizing data security and integrity initiatives. Their support is key to allocating resources, standardizing practices, and fostering a culture that values data as a trustworthy and protected asset.

Encourage leaders to champion the alignment of security and integrity efforts, recognizing that strong data security practices inherently support the goal of maintaining high data quality and reliability.
